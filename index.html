Into the Storm


A drowning sun cast its last light across from the other side of the horizon, some of it ricocheting across the massive clouds whirling around the sun, others shimmering and scattering across the ocean surface, reaching through the tall rock pillars, out to the edge of the cliffs, where Anya stood.

The pastel-orange glow of the sun gently washed over her, all while an ocean breeze greeted her with a salty and earthly aroma. Peering into the fading sky, she closed her eyes and dug her bare feet into the loose ground, She was part of a symphony that evening; there was the loud crashing of the waves, the distant warbling of the gulls, a soft whistle of the winds and maybe some rustling of the leaves behind her. An amphitheatre, with her as the audience and sun as the poor conductor.

She wished she were still there. Never wanting to leave, never letting go.

It was nice.

A Brazen horn blared above, and Anya woke leaning against the cold steel of the carriage. In a haze, she heard the speaker announce, but couldn’t decipher what it was trying to say. Looking around, she saw some people get up, and exit the carriage. Anya looked at her watch again — no, it wasn’t her time yet. Irritated, by a second blaring of the speaker, she shifted to one of the now empty seats. It wouldn’t matter, they had designed them so the speakers were evenly placed across the train, above the horizontal window slits.

Anya had no idea where she was, or her destination, only that they had asked her to exit when the digits on her watch numbered zero. Bored, stuck in an empty steel coffin, bathed in an awful low white light, she tried to go back to sleep again but it wouldn’t come. Her body could only rest so much, She had had more than her fair share of sleep, so much so that she had started to experience pain in her temples and pressure on her chest.

“Fine”

To entertain, Anya started to count the number of many people waiting for their own destinations, hoping to fall asleep. “Not enough sheep” she muttered disdainfully to herself. Her bored glances fell to the floor, which was as dull as any other piece of that train — grey, metallic, and plain, barring of course the light strip that had guided each passenger to their seat. Besides them, there were the passing shades of grey that fell on the carriage floor from the top “window” slits, punctuated by brief flashes of light, followed by loud crashes of thunder. It was raining — It always rained, all day and all night — she could always hear hard tappers of rain hitting against the metal. And for those fleeting moments, when it didn’t rain, the shadows resembled canopies of trees, it reminded her of the time she spent building wooden fences with her father. Every summer come, they would go fishing and collect driftwood. Those were some bitter-sweet days.

Anya missed her father dearly. “I wonder if the house is still there” she muttered to no one in particular. she hoped somebody would notice. No one talked to each other or even showed any inkling of such. How long had it been? weeks, maybe even a month, she couldn’t tell — her watch was broke. Time was one commodity they all had plenty of, so why? — Was it fear? was it that they had nothing to talk about? The silence was deafening.

She wanted to go home, she wished she hadn’t left.

Another station came, and she looked at her watch again.

52

What 52? she vexed, what do the numbers mean? From the moment she had gotten on the train, her watch had shown a different number upon reaching each station, in no exact pattern. Anya had studied high school level math, she thought she could at least figure out some rudimentary pattern; sometimes she felt she had it, other times the numbers just seemed too random. She reminded herself that it was of no use, and strayed into other thoughts. Didn’t matter how many stations came to pass, The numbers on her watch never seemed to hit zero.

Closing her eyes yet again she tried to go back to sleep.
